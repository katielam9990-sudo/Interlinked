# Interlinked — Workshopped Prompts

Working doc for prompts developed against the Prompt Workshop Sheet. Each prompt is written as a full unit — prompt line, seeds, stars, help questions — with the bridge shapes it supports noted underneath.

## Prompt 1 — Speeding Up vs Slowing Down
*Status: draft — one open decision (final prompt line)*

**Theme:** the trade between pace and presence — what speeding up costs, what slowing down reveals

**Prompt** *(pick one — read aloud, keep the one that makes you start remembering):*
> **A.** Name the season you couldn't slow down — and the day you did.
>
> **B.** You slowed down once and saw something you'd been outrunning. What were you speeding toward before that?

**Seeds** *(reseeded to break the antonym-obviousness — the two poles no longer just define each other):*

- **Seed 1 (on-ramp):** something you were chasing
- **Seed 2 (the harder star):** something you only saw once you'd stopped

**Star labels:**

- **Star 1:** *what you were speeding toward*
- **Star 2:** *what you finally saw when you stopped*

**Example stars** *(rough on purpose — sets altitude without looking unreachable):*

- Star 1: *the two years I said yes to everything to make partner*
- Star 2: *the waiting room where I finally had to sit still*

**Help questions** *(specific first, one at the intersection, one self-distancing):*

1. At what point did you decide you had to change pace? *(when → pulls a scene)*
2. Did slowing down teach you how to speed up, or the other way around? *(aimed at the intersection)*
3. Who or what was pushing the pace? *(names the source — replaces the abstract "what pressures you")*
4. Who were you in each moment? *(self-distancing / identity)*

**Bridge shapes supported:** trade-off (the exchange) · causal (the turning point) · common root (the source). Three-bridges test passes.

**Open decision:** choose between prompt line A (shortest, most inscription-like) and B (the "outrunning" verb quietly names the trade without spending it).

## Prompt 2 — The Power of Culture
*Status: unit ready*

**Theme:** the inherited script for how you're supposed to live *(named on the creator card, never in the prompt)*

**Prompt:**
> Someone handed you a script for how life was supposed to go. When did you first go off it?

**Seeds:**

- **Seed 1 (on-ramp):** the life the script called for
- **Seed 2 (the harder star):** the moment you first went off it

**Star labels:**

- **Star 1:** *the life you were supposed to want*
- **Star 2:** *the moment you went off-script*

**Example stars** *(rough on purpose):*

- Star 1: *steady job, married by 28, kids before the parents got old*
- Star 2: *the night I said I was quitting the plan, out loud at dinner, and no one spoke*

**Help questions:**

1. Who wrote the script — a parent, a religion, a whole hometown? *(most specific; names the vessel)*
2. The moment you went off it, whose voice did you hear first? *(aimed at the intersection — hands users the common-root shape)*
3. Have you ever envied someone whose life looked "wrong" by your script? *(self-distancing; kills the pre-cached answer)*

**Bridge shapes supported:** trade-off (the right life cost you the freedom the "wrong" ones had) · causal (one moment cracked the script) · common root (script and rebellion trace to the same standard). Three-bridges test passes.

**Creator card (meaning after):** name "the power of culture" out loud here — the recognition that the script was never neutral, it was handed to you — plus the source material that sparked the prompt. Not before the bridge.

## Prompt 3 — The Self You'll Never Be (Shadow / Non-Shadow)
*Status: ready — one helper swapped from the draft (see note on HQ 3)*

**Theme:** self-limiting beliefs, both directions — the disowned *better* self and the disowned *shadow*. The discovery is a capacity you edited out of your self-image to keep it clean. On-brand with the brief's yearning ("understand themselves better") and its moral ("witness").

**Prompt:**
> There is a version of yourself that you decided you'll never be. When did you almost prove yourself wrong?

**Seeds:**

- **Seed 1 (on-ramp):** something you've decided you're "not the type" to do
- **Seed 2 (the harder star):** a moment that almost proved otherwise

**Star labels:**

- **Star 1:** *the person you're sure you're not*
- **Star 2:** *the moment you almost were*

**Example stars** *(a matched shadow-leaning pair — targets a disowned feeling, not an act, so there's a low-shame way in; can run aspirational just as easily):*

- Star 1: *someone who'd never cut a real friend out of their life*
- Star 2: *the night I noticed I felt relief that we'd stopped speaking*

**Help questions** *(specific first, one at the intersection, self-distancing throughout):*

1. What did you do the last time someone suggested you try something out of character? *(most specific; ties to Seed 1)*
2. Who first told you that you weren't that kind of person? *(self-distancing; traces the story to its source)*
3. The moment it almost happened, how did you talk yourself back out of it? *(the intersection — the stickiness of the self-image; works in both directions. Swapped in for the original "fair chance" question, which pulled a verdict instead of a scene.)*
4. When did you catch yourself almost crossing a line you swore you'd never cross? *(the shadow direction; "catch yourself" is self-distancing, "almost" keeps it a felt pull, not a confession)*

**Bridge shapes supported:** compensatory (the self-image protects you from the disowned capacity — bridge = the origin story) · causal (the near-miss cracked the certainty) · common root (the self-image and the disowned part trace to the same source). Three-bridges test passes.

**Safety by design (this prompt sits closest to the static-wound risk, §6):** tone is *capacity, not guilt* — "you contain more than your self-image admits," which is fascinating, not damning; the "almost" / near-miss framing throughout keeps the shadow side a felt pull rather than a deed to confess; the movement (self-image → the moment it cracked) stops it becoming a wound to circle.

**Creator card (meaning after):** name it here — the self-image edits out both your best and your grey, and witnessing the edited-out part is self-knowledge, not judgment. This is the brief's "witness" moral, exactly.
